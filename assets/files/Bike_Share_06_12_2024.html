Analysis on memberships for bike share

The data set we’re working with was provided by Lyft Bikes and Scooters, LLC (“Bikeshare”) with intention of analyses for non-commercial purposes.

Some preliminary definitions for the data are:

Term Definition
Casual User Users who use single-ride or day-passes
Annual Members Users who purchase annual memberships

The goal of this case study is to use the data provided to provide insight on how to improve the profitability of the bike share service. Thus the business task we’re going to tackle is:

Design marketing strategies aimed at converting casual riders into annual members

With the business task define this leads to some questions to help us tackle the task. Some questions that come to mind are:

  • In what way do members and casual users differ when it comes to the usage of the service?
  • Why would a casual user buy an annual membership?
  • How do we influence a casual user to become a member?

Data Specifications

The data set we’re using spans from Jan 24th 2020 to Jun 3rd 2024 and tracks bike share usage in the City of Chicago. Given the service is one that requires people to go outside to utilize it some consideration must be made for the years of COVID-19 where the service usage would have been impacted from the years 2020 to 2021. To alleviate this issue I’ll pull the data from the available Jun 8th 2023 to Jun 3rd 2024 to account for data in a year where stay-at-home orders wasn’t in effect for the City of Chicago as well as making sure the data is as recent as possible (while still being sizable).

This pivot table here gives us some interesting metrics to work with. Casuals in general seem to prefer weekends with longer rides on those days while Members also prefer weekends but overall prefer shorter rides. While both members and casuals have instances of accidentally taking out bikes casuals seem to take bikes out for the longest rides whereas the longest ride is likely forgetting to return a bike, for reference 604800 is one week in seconds so a casual user taking out a book for 9.7 weeks likely seems like a mistake.

Additionally we see our metric of “two minute or less” rides are different between casuals and members but the difference is quite minuscule overall with a 3% to 4% increase between casuals to members for these short rides.

Presentation and Recommendations gleaned from our data

Lets go ahead and restate our questions one last time, this time using our insights to inform some answers then use the export and answers to tell a story using our data.

Design marketing strategies aimed at converting casual riders into annual members We address this business task by looking at what differs between causal and annual members and how we can show off the benefits of being a member to a casual consumer to try and convert them.

  • In what way do members and casual users differ when it comes to the usage of the service? Members and casuals differ in both frequency and time of usage. A member is more likely to use the service on non-weekend days especially during early morning hours and after work evening hours.

Additionally the member is not pressured like a casual user is when it comes to the full utilization the limited nature of a casual user single ride or day pass and thus can ride as long or as short as they want. Unlike a casual user who tends to ride for much longer.

Both users are similar in that generally in the evening and non-winter months is when the most utilization happens.

  • Why would a casual user buy an annual membership? A casual user would buy an annual membership to use the bike more overall throughout the year. With special focus on being able to use them during weekdays or even during winter months. Additionally they’re able to use the bikes during more hours of the day. Namely they can ride both early in the morning then later in the evening without needing two seperate payments of single-ride in between.

  • How do we influence a casual user to become a member? Advertise both the ability to ride bikes during non weekend times and being able to ride at their own leisure. A casual user is pressured to carry the bike for longer because of the nature of the single ride or day pass. While a member is more versatile they can use a bike to get to work or school then drop it off until they need it again later in the day.

  • Do members use the bikes more than casuals in: Peak Hours/Season/Day of the Week? Yes members overall use the bikes more often during all of peak hours, season, and day of the week. Especially relevant is the usage of the bikes during the 7 am and 5 pm time slots where they’re used quite a bit.

  • Do members use the bikes for longer duration than casual users? No, in fact the casual seem to use the bikes for much longer than members

  • Is the ability to rent bikes for small duration useful as a member? Yes, members tend to want to utilize the bike multiple times in a day rather than one long trip with the average trip being much shorter for members than casuals (about half as long).

To summarize our data story we want to tell is that: To tackle the design marketing strategy we should advertise the way members are able to utilize the service much more versatile ways. Such as being able to take short rides whenever they need it, taking rides during morning to get to work then later in the evening to come back from work, or utilize the bike during winter months if they so choose.
